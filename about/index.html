  • vocal synth production;

    I suppose this technically falls under music production, but considering the scope of the hobby, I chose to make it a separate bullet point. Vocal synths, for the uninitiated, are basically computer-generated singers. When dedicated singing synthesizers came out, they originally used a bunch of human voice sounds that get strung together to make them sing the appropriate sounds, but nowadays, we have AI-generated singers as well. I'm over simplifying it here though, because this page is about me. I started using a program called Vocaloid on August 2, 2022. No, I don't remember the date by heart. I know this date because I still have the first project I ever created on my computer. Anyway, as you can probably imagine, I wasn't particularly good at using Vocaloid when I first started out. What may surprize a lot of people is how quickly I learned the program. By August 8, I learned a majority of the program's features and how to use them. I knew how to work with Vocaloid. Within 3 months, I was able to master the entire program, including all the subtle tricks that made the voices sound better. I knew how to make Vocaloid work for me. In December of 2023, I also began learning how to use one of those AI programs I mentioned above (Synthesizer V) and figured it out in about a week. I know; I'm bragging. I have a good excuse for that though. You see, I have a lot of hobbies, but this particular hobby took the least amount of time for me to master. It also took me less time to master Vocaloid than it did for many of the fantastic producers we've got now. You also have to remember that I am blind, and when it comes to information about Vocaloid and vocal synth technology in the blind community in terms of how well we can use it, there's not a whole lot of public knowledge about the subject. In fact, it saddens me that I might be the only blind person in the world who knows as much about vocal synth technology usage as I do, and I worry every day what's going to happen to that information when I'm gone. But don't worry. I'm not special, and I don't believe I am special or better than anyone else. I'm just very proud of how quickly I was able to learn this particular hobby, because it's one of the few hobbies I think I'm really good at and have confidence in.

Something that seems to surprise a lot of people about me is the fact that I am blind. Technically I would be refered to by most people as visually impaired, because I can see lights and shadows. However, since that is all I can see, the vision I do have is mostly useless. Because of that, I call myself blind. The reason people are surprized is because, even in 2024, a lot of them still don't know that blind people are capable of using technology of any kind. Alass, I absolutely can. I can do almost everything sighted people can do, thanks to a nifty invention called a screen reader. And the fact is, I'm nobody special. Thousands of blind people do most of the things I do every single day. Unfortunately, not everything is a walk in the park for us. You see, developers have to put in the effort to make their application accessible to screen readers. A lot of developers do put in the effort, or they use certain libraries in their applications that take care of the screen reader accessibility part for them. There are a lot of developers, however, who don't make their programs accessible for us. This is usually because, A, the developer works for a company that doesn't really care about us, or B, because they are an indi developer who don't know how to do it. Case in point, I use a program called Vocaloid. This program is not fully usable with screen readers, so I've had to find inventive ways around the problem.
